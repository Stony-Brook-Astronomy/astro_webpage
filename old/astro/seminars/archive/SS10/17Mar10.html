Title : "Mid-Infrared Silicate Variability in Actively Accreting T Tauri Stars"
Speaker : Jeff Bary, Colgate

During the cold mission of the Spitzer Space Telescope, hundreds of high sensitivity mid-infrared spectra of T Tauri stars were collected with the Infrared Spectrograph (IRS).  Many of these spectra detected emission from super-heated silicate dust grains most likely located in the upper atmospheres of the inner regions of the proto-planetary disks surrounding these young Sun-like stars.  Performing detailed studies of the 10 um silicate feature, several groups have found source-to-source variations in the morphology and structure of the silicate emission complex.  These variations are likely to be caused by differences in the composition and structure of their respective dust grain populations.  Since observations of dust grains in the interstellar medium and solar system objects demonstrate that dust grain populations evolve from disorganized, amorphous grains to annealed, organized crystalline structures, previous observers concluded that the source-to-source variations of the amorphous and crystalline mass fractions hint at a possible disk evolutionary sequence (i.e., larger amorphous mass fraction equals a less evolved disk).  In order to test this evolutionary sequence hypothesis, we conducted a variability study of 11 actively accreting T Tauri stars possessing optically thick disks to determine if the source-to-source variations are not due to intrinsic, short timescale variability of the silicate emission features.  For two of our sources, we find significant variability in the strength of the feature on month-long timescales.  For the others, we see only small fluctuations in the crystalline and amorphous mass fractions.  I will review the results of our observational program, offer some physical explanations for the observed phenomena, and compare the results of our program to those from a similar multi-epoch IRS programs.
