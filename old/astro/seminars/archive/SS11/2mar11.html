Title : Simulating and Examining the First Stars in the Universe
Speaker : Matthew Turk (Columbia University)

The concordance model of the first stars in the Universe for the last decade contained the central thesis that these stars were massive, formed in isolation, and were but a brief moment in the history of the universe. However, recent advances in microphysical modeling and simulation sophistication suggest that this may not be the case. We discuss a cosmological simulation in which the central 50 solar-mass clump breaks up into two cores, having a mass ratio of two to one, which will likely form a binary star system. Additionally, we will present ongoing work to sample a number of primordial star-forming halos, to constrain the initial mass function of these first stars. We discuss the formation and settling of accretion disks, mechanisms for terminating accretion, the incidence of multiples, and potential further mechanisms for constraining and examining fragmentation. We conclude with a look toward possible future work for understanding the initial mass function of the very first stars to form in the Universe.
