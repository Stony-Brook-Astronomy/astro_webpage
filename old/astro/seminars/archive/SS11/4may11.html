Title : Spitzer View of Massive Star Formation in the Magellanic System
Speaker : Rosie Chen (University of Virginia)

Massive stars drive the evolution of the interstellar medium and hence play an important role in the evolution of their host galaxies, yet their formation remains poorly understood. Recent Spitzer mid-IR surveys reveal a large number of individually resolved massive young stellar objects (YSOs) in the Magellanic Clouds, providing an excellent opportunity to study massive star formation with metallicity and galactic environment different from the Milky Way. We have used Spitzer IRAC/MIPS and complementary near-IR and optical data to study massive YSOs in HII complexes in the Large and Small Magellanic Cloud (LMC and SMC) and the high HI column density, N(HI), portion of the Magellanic Bridge; these systems span a metallicity range of 1/3-1/8 Zo, and a range of galactic environment from minor interacting in the LMC to tidally disrupted in the Bridge. To compare properties of YSOs in these systems, we have modeled their spectral energy distributions (SEDs), and found that their masses range from 4 to 45 Mo in the LMC and SMC while the most massive ones are only ~10 Mo in the Bridge. Furthermore, for the same mass range, YSOs in the Bridge appear less embedded as almost all of them are visible at optical, opposed to only a small fraction in the LMC. The smaller extinction is likely due to the Bridge's lower dust content in the molecular clouds or circumstellar envelopes, or both. To assess the metallicity or environmental effect on large-scale star formation, we have used complete samples of YSOs in the LMC and the Bridge to estimate the probability of finding YSOs at a given N(HI). We found that the LMC has ~3 times higher probability than the Bridge for N(HI) > 10x10^20 cm^-2, but the trend reverses at lower N(HI). Different formation mechanisms of molecular clouds, such as colliding flows, are likely to be responsible for the reverse trend.
