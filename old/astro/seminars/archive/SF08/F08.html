Nov. 20*Erin Sheldon
BNL		 Tests of Cold Dark Matter from Gravitational Lensing in the SDSS
The cold dark matter model, in the context of a hot big bang, makes definite predictions about the formation of structures in our universe, but predicts nothing about some fundamental quantities such as the mean mass density or the relative amount of various material constituents. In CDM, bound objects have a great deal of regularity in their internal structure, a so-called universal mass profile, and the relative spatial distribution of these objects is determined almost entirely by their mass. However, the actual number of objects of a given mass depends on free parameters such as the mean and variance of the mass field. I'll show measurements of the gravitational lensing effect in the Sloan Digital Sky Survey that simultaneously test predictions of the CDM and measure free parameters. I'll present measurements of the mass profiles of galaxy clusters, as well as large-scale cross-correlations between these objects and the mass field, both of which are predictions of the model. I'll also show measurements of the relative spatial distribution of light and mass around clusters over three orders of magnitude in scale, and use this to infer free parameters of the model: the mean mass-to-light ratio of the universe and the total mass density.

Dec. 3David Lafreniere
Toronto		Great catches in hunt for exoplanets: the first direct images!
Since the historic discovery of 51 Peg b in 1995, over 300 exoplanets have been discovered, indirectly, through the subtle effects that they induce on their parent stars. These discoveries have revealed an unexpected diversity of ?exo-worlds? and brought forth crucial information to advance our understanding of how planets form and evolve around stars. The picture remains incomplete, however, as planets on orbits the size of our solar system or larger cannot be detected using these indirect techniques, and accurate characterization of planets found in this manner is very difficult. Direct imaging detection is currently our only means to complete the picture, and would offer new possibilities to characterize the planets in more details. Fortunately, huge first steps in this direction have just been made, with the direct imaging detections of a distant planetary mass candidate companion to a very young star in Upper Scorpius, three massive planets in orbit around the young star HR 8799, and a planet orbiting inside the dust belt of the bright star Fomalhaut. In this talk I will review each of these discoveries and briefly discuss their potential impact on our understanding of planet formation.
