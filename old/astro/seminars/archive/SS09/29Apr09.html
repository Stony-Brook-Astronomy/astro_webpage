Title : The Astrochemical Evolution of Turbulent Giant Molecular Clouds
Speaker : Robert Fisher, University of Massachusetts at Dartmouth

Contemporary galactic star formation occurs predominantly within gravitationally unstable, cold, dense molecular gas within supersonic, turbulent, magnetized giant molecular clouds (GMCs). Significantly, because the gas phase ion-neutral chemical evolution timescale and the turbulent eddy-turnover timescale are comparable at typical GMC conditions, molecules evolve via an inherently non-equilibrium chemistry which is strongly coupled to the dynamical evolution of the cloud. As a result, GMC molecular abundances are powerful diagnostics of its underlying dynamics, and are crucial in interpreting observations, as well as in constraining theoretical models of GMCs and star formation. However, modeling the post-shock cooling region while also fully simulating the global dynamics of a turbulent supersonic clump presents a significant challenge to any 3D simulation, even with the power of adaptive mesh refinement. The postshock cooling region behind a hydrodynamic jump or "J-shock" is of order 1 AU in thickness, and a magnetized continuous or "C-shock" of order 10 AU thickness under typical GMC clump conditions. In contrast, a supersonic turbulent GMC clump is of order 1 pc in size, resulting in a dynamic range of four to five decades in length scale between the largest and smallest scales required to simultaneously model both supersonic turbulence and molecular shock chemistry.

We are currently addressing the challenge of the GMC astrochemical scales problem using a novel solution methodology, which permits both complex three-dimensional turbulent dynamics as well as accurate treatment of non-equilibrium post-shock thermodynamics and chemistry. Significantly, the ability to compare realistic astrochemical models of turbulent GMC models against observation promises to expand our diagnostic windows onto the physics of GMCs. In particular, these models will soon help to elucidate the role of essential aspects of the star formation process, including the role of both the magnetic field and turbulence, as well as to shed light on new mechanisms for the formation of complex organic molecules.
