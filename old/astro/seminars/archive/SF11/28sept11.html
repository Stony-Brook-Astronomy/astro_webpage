Title: Solution Accurate, Efficient and Stable Unsplit Hydrodynamics and Magnetohydrodynamics Solvers in FLASH
Speaker: Dongwook Lee (University of Chicago)

FLASH is publicly available astrophysical community code designed to solve highly compressible multi-physics reactive flows. It supports a wide range of physical capabilities by combining interoperable modules to simulate problems such as novae, supernovae, X-ray bursts, galaxy clusters, weakly compressible turbulence, and many other problems in astrophysics and other fields.

Numerical solvers in FLASH are finite-volume, high-order Godunov Riemann solvers for gas dynamics, magnetohydrodynamics, and special relativistic hydrodynamics. In this talk, I will focus on a new set of two unsplit solvers in FLASH for gas dynamics and magnetohydrodynamics.

The new unsplit solvers follow the basic ideas of the 2D corner-transport-upwind (CTU) unsplit algorithm by Colella and its 3D full CTU extension by Saltzman (aka, 12-Riemann solver for 3D). The key difference in our unsplit algorithm is to implement transverse fluxes that stabilize an unsplit hyperbolic system without solving separate intermediate Riemann problems. The scheme instead uses multidimensional characteristic tracing to account for the stabilizing transverse fluxes, only requiring three Riemann solves in 3D rather than 12 Riemann solves in the full CTU scheme. The proposed algorithm is also simple and efficient to include multidimensional MHD source terms that are important to maintain in-plane magnetic field dynamics for MHD. The new 3D unsplit solvers provide a CFL limit close to unity in most of test problems available in literatures.

The new unsplit solvers can provide flexibility and extensibility to allow users to add additional physics to the system such as fluid viscosity, heat conduction, magnetic diffusivity, gravity, cooling, nuclear burning, and a mechanism to support different kinds of equations of state.

Finally I will briefly talk about new FLASH's efforts to develop high-energy-density physics implementations to simulate long pulse laser direct drive experiments.
