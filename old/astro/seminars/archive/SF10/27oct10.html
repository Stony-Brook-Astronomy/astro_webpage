Title : What are the Peculiar Emission Line Profiles of Recurrent Novae Trying to Tell Us?
Speaker : Fred Walter

The novae are thermonuclear explosions on the surfaces of white dwarf stars, generally in close binary systems. The recurrent novae are those novae which have been observed to explode more than once in human history. Since the explosion occurs when the accreted hydrogen layer becomes degenerate and hot, a short time interval implies the white dwarf is near-Chandrasekhar mass. The optical characteristics of the recurrent novae clearly differentiate them from the more numerous classical novae, in terms of speed of decline, spectral line profiles, and apparent composition. I have started a project to obtain long term, high cadence photometry and spectroscopy of southern novae using the SMARTS facilities. After a general introduction to novae, I shall turn to the peculiar line profiles of the recurrent novae and the look-alike He-Ne novae. I interpret the line profiles in these systems not in terms of an expanding equatorial torus, but in terms of an accretion disk. I shall try to show that the spectral evolution is consistent with the rapid re-establishment of the disk after (or survival through) the nova explosion.
