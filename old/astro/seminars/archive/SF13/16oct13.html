Observing Coalescence of Neutron Stars and Black Holes
Speaker : Ruslan Vaulin (MIT)

Binary neutron stars and/or stellar-mass black holes are unique laboratories for strong-field gravity and high-energy astrophysics. Coalescence of these extreme objects are among the most energetic cataclysmic astrophysical events in the universe, emitting most of their energy in the form of gravitational-wave (GW) radiation. They are also believed to be progenitors of short gamma-ray bursts, r-process radiation and other electromagnetic transients. Due to their transitive nature, up until now direct observations of these systems have been virtually impossible except via their beamed gamma-ray emission which could not provide unequivocal identification. In my talk I will describe ongoing efforts to employ the network of advanced LIGO and Virgo detectors (to become operational in 2015) for detection of GW radiation from coalescing compact binaries. GW data combined with results of coordinated follow-up observations and independent transient surveys across broad EM spectrum, from radio to gamma-ray, will provide us with complete picture of the process of coalescence. I will discuss some of the main challenges in carrying out such observations and required data analyses. In particular, I will talk about observations of orphan afterglows using low-frequency widefield radio arrays such as Murchison Widefield Array (MWA). I will conclude with discussion of prospects for routine observations of GW and EM signals from coalescing binaries in the second half of this decade.
