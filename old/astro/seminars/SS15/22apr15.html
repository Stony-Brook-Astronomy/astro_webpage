Investigating the physics of core-collapse supernovae and ultra-relativistic outflows using pulsar wind nebulae
Joseph Gelfand (NYU)

The bulk of a neutron star's rotational energy is believed to power an ultra-relativistic outflow, called a pulsar wind, which creates a pulsar wind nebula (PWN) when it expands into its surrounding. When the neutron star is young, it is located within the supernova remnant (SNR) created by the progenitor explosion. In this case, the evolution of the PWN is sensitive to that of the evolution of both the neutron star and supernova ejecta. Additionally, the association of many Galactic gamma-ray sources with young pulsars strongly suggests that particles are accelerated to extremely high energy in these sources. In this talk, I will demonstrate how the properties of a PWN inside a SNR can be used to measure the properties of the central neutron stars, its pulsar wind, and progenitor supernova -- providing important information regarding the formation of neutron stars and high-energy cosmic ray electrons.
