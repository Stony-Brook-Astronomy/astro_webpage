Finding and focusing the best cosmic telescopes
Chuck Keeton (Rutgers)

Studying galaxy formation in the young universe is one of the frontiers of observational cosmology. Blank field surveys have detected galaxy candidates at high redshift, but only with large investments of telescope time. Gravitational lensing by galaxy clusters can magnify distant sources and make them easier to detect, but it also reduces the area surveyed. Our team has predicted that the beams with the largest etendue (area in which sources are magnified) are likely to contain multiple cluster-scale halos. We have used projected concentrations of Luminous Red Galaxies to identify promising beams, and begun follow-up spectroscopy to characterize them in detail. The first two beams we have examined each contain at least two group- or cluster-scale halos, and mass models based on dynamical data suggest that they are at least as powerful as the HST Frontier Fields. In one beam we found a multiply-imaged V-band dropout source with a photometric redshift z=5.03. We are now building lens models that combine pixel-based source reconstruction with a full three-dimensional treatment of the lens equation. These high-etendue beams have the potential to become legacy-class lensing fields that will be used into the era of JWST and 30m-class telescopes to boost faint sources into detectability.
