Neutron Star physics with Gravitational Wave astronomy: the importance of being magnetic
Simone Dall'Osso (U. of Tubingen)

The forthcoming generation of laser interferometers will be able to reveal for the first time the expected GW signals from a wide variety of cosmic sources, thus opening the window of gravitational wave astronomy. Neutron stars are among the most interesting targets of this exciting new field. Quite generally their GW signals will allow to probe aspects of their physics/astrophysics hardly accessible by other means, such as the violent dynamics in the inner regions of core-collapse supernovae, the structure and microphysics of the ultra dense NS cores, or the very spot where NS/BHs collide liberating huge amounts of energy, and leaving a compact remnant whose nature in largely influenced by the NS eos. I will illustrate general mechanisms through which a NS magnetic field can be critical in promoting efficient GW emission, and discuss selected examples of the most interesting astrophysical cases where these mechanisms can be at work. The GW signals have, in general, specific features that make them well recognisable and can be "easily" associated with the main physics parameters of NSs, including of course the strength (and shape) of their interior magnetic field, and the stiffness of their equation of state. Conditions for the emission of these GW signals are such that specific electromagnetic counterparts are also expected. Hence, complementary information can be gathered from observations of high-energy radiation, making a strong case for a multi messenger approach to study NSs in the present and near future.
