The X-ray view of black hole winds in Active Galactic Nuclei and their importance for galaxy evolution
Francesco Tombesi (U. of Maryland)

Our X-ray view of winds in Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN) has significantly improved in recent years. In this talk I will briefly review some of the main observational results in this field. For instance, supporting evidences for powerful ultra-fast outflows (UFOs) have been reported through the detection of highly ionized and blue-shifted Fe K absorption lines with velocities up to ~10-30% the speed of light. Their characteristics suggest a direct identification with winds ejected from the inner accretion disk surrounding the central super-massive black hole (SMBH). Significant correlations between the parameters of these UFOs and larger-scale ionized absorbers have been reported, suggesting their possible unification as a single, large-scale stratified outflow observed at different locations along the line of sight, from the vicinity of the central SMBH up to the outskirts of the AGN host galaxy. Their detailed acceleration mechanism(s) are still debated, but observations and theoretical results favor a combination of radiation pressure and/or MHD processes. The deposition of mass and energy of these winds in the AGN host galaxy can be substantial and, indeed, they may represent the long sought-after agent mediating the "quasar mode" feedback in SMBH-galaxy evolution. Finally, I will show simulations of the improvements in these studies that will come from the unprecedented Fe K energy resolution and sensitivity of the calorimeters onboard the upcoming space based JAXA/NASA ASTRO-H X-ray mission (launch date at the end of 2015) and the future ESA Athena X-ray observatory (launch date 2028).
