Title : The Youngest Planets and Spots' Tricks
Speaker : Lisa Prato, Lowell Observatory

A correlation between the radial velocity variations and line bisector slope changes in late-type stars implicates large starspots rather than a low-mass companion. Two young stars, and recent results in the literature, demonstrate that the absence of this correlation is insufficient to rule out starspots. As part of our survey for substellar companions to T Tauri stars, we identified the ~2 Myr old planet host candidates DN Tau and V836 Tau. In both cases, visible light radial velocity modulation appears periodic and is uncorrelated with line bisector span variations, suggesting close companions of several Jupiter masses in these systems. However, high-resolution, infrared spectroscopy demonstrated that these radial velocity variations arise in starspots. Observations of another young star, V827 Tau, are unambiguous: it is identified as a spotted star on the basis of both visible light and infrared spectroscopy. These results suggest that infrared follow up observations are critical for determining the source of radial velocity modulation in young, spotted stars. I will highlight recent observations of a promising ~2 Myr exoplanet host candidate which manifests a similar radial velocity period and amplitude in both visible and infrared light.
